the problem

A cane only tells you something is there once you've almost hit it.

Over 250 million people worldwide live with significant vision loss. Most rely on a cane or guide dog — tools that detect obstacles only at arm's length, and tell you nothing about what the obstacle actually is.

There's no affordable, everyday device that tells a blind person what is ahead, how far, and which way to move — in their own language, hands-free, in real time.

the idea

Same camera as Ray-Ban Meta or Snap Spectacles — pointed at a real problem.

Camera glasses already exist as a consumer product. This project applies that exact hardware concept — a lightweight, always-forward-facing camera — to accessibility instead of social media: see the world, understand it with AI, and speak it out loud.

architecture

Five files, one pipeline.

FileRole
app.pyFlask server — opens the camera, streams annotated video, exposes settings and status endpoints.
detector.pyRuns YOLOv8n on each frame, extracts labels/confidence/bounding boxes, draws the overlay.
danger_logic.pyRule-based reasoning — zones, distance estimate, urgency scoring, one final instruction.
voice.pyConverts the instruction to speech, multilingual, with repeat-cooldown.
templates / staticWeb dashboard — live feed, detected objects, voice and language controls.

how danger is decided

Zone, distance, and object type — no depth sensor required.

The frame is split into three zones. Bounding-box size approximates distance. Vehicles and center-zone objects are weighted higher — only the single most urgent object is spoken aloud.

multilingual voice

Spoken alerts, not just detections.

Offline voice works without internet. Online multilingual mode covers Telugu, Hindi, Marathi, Gujarati, Tamil, Kannada, Malayalam, Bengali, Urdu, Arabic, French, Spanish, German, and more.

Repeats are suppressed with a cooldown, so the same warning isn't spoken every single frame.
